Why Do Orbits Close?

A 150-year-old theorem reveals that only two force laws produce orbits that retrace themselves. Every gravitational system in the universe can be understood as a departure from these two perfect cases.

An interactive guide to Bertrand's theorem and the β parameter

Drop a ball and it falls. Throw it sideways and it curves. Throw it fast enough and it orbits. But remarkably for almost every force law you might conceive, such an orbit would never close as the particle traces a path that fills an annular region, never exactly retracing itself. Nature selects the two force laws in all of mathematics that produce orbits that close perfectly, every time, at every amplitude.

This is the content of Bertrand's theorem, proved in 1873. The two privileged force laws are the inverse-square law (gravity as we know it, $f \propto 1/r^2$) and the harmonic restoring force ($f \propto r$, the force inside a uniform sphere of matter). Every other central force produces orbits that precess in rotating slowly, never quite closing.

We reinterpret this classical result through its single parameter, $\beta$, defined as the ratio of the radial oscillation frequency to the orbital frequency. When $\beta$ is rational, orbits close; when it takes one of two special values ($\beta = 1$ or $\beta = 2$), they close at every amplitude. The entire landscape of gravitational dynamics: from the innermost stable orbit of a black hole to the expansion of the universe. can be mapped as a journey through $\beta$-space.

Part I

The Breathing Orbit

To build intuition for $\beta$, consider what happens to the moment of inertia $I = mr^2$ as a particle orbits. For a circular orbit, $I$ is constant. For an eccentric orbit, the particle swings between a closest approach (periapsis) and farthest point (apoapsis), and $I$ oscillates as the orbit breathes.

The frequency of this breathing is $\beta$ times the orbital frequency. For the Kepler problem ($\beta = 1$), the system breathes once per orbit: one expansion, one contraction. For the harmonic oscillator ($\beta = 2$), it breathes twice. For a general force law, $\beta$ can take any value, and the orbit traces a rosette.

Orbital Rosettes and Breathing

Left: orbital trace in position space. Right: moment of inertia $I(\theta)$ showing the breathing oscillation. For $\beta = 1$ (Kepler), the orbit closes after one revolution. For irrational $\beta$, it never closes.

The Kepler orbit ($\beta = 1$) is the one we know from planetary motion: a closed ellipse, retraced identically on every revolution. The harmonic oscillator ($\beta = 2$) produces a centred ellipse in which the particle passes through the force centre twice per orbit, and the breathing is twice as fast. The $\beta = \sqrt{2}$ case (logarithmic potential, flat rotation curves) produces a rosette that never closes, since $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational. And when $\beta < 1$, the periapsis advances, being the precession that Einstein explained for Mercury.

Part II

The Cubic Constraint

Orbital closure at the linear level requires only that $\beta$ be rational. But Bertrand's theorem demands more: closure must hold at all amplitudes, not just for nearly circular orbits. When the perturbation analysis is pushed to third order, the nonlinear terms impose a devastating constraint:

The Bertrand Cubic
$$\beta^2(1 - \beta^2)(4 - \beta^2) = 0$$

The three roots are $\beta^2 = 0$, $\beta^2 = 1$, and $\beta^2 = 4$. The first ($\beta = 0$) is marginal: it corresponds to $f \propto r^{-3}$, where the linear restoring force vanishes entirely. The other two are the physical solutions. For a power-law force $f \propto r^p$, the connection is:

Force exponent to frequency ratio
$$\beta^2 = p + 3$$

So $p = -2$ (inverse-square) gives $\beta^2 = 1$; $p = +1$ (Hooke's law) gives $\beta^2 = 4$, hence $\beta = 2$. These are the only two. Every other power law either fails the nonlinear test or sits at the degenerate $\beta = 0$ boundary.

Part III

The Kottler Mesa

In the real universe, a gravitating mass does not sit in empty flat space. It sits in a universe with a cosmological constant $\Lambda > 0$. The exact solution for a point mass in a $\Lambda$ background was found by Friedrich Kottler in 1918 — the Schwarzschild–de Sitter metric. The effective potential has four terms:

Kottler effective potential
$$V_{\text{eff}}(r) = -\frac{GMm}{r} + \frac{L^2}{2mr^2} - \frac{GML^2}{c^2 m r^3} - \frac{\Lambda c^2}{6}\,r^2$$

Each term dominates at a different scale, and from this single expression we can compute $\beta^2(r)$ at every radius. The result has the shape of a mesa: a flat-topped plateau near $\beta^2 = 1$ (the Kepler regime) bounded by two cliffs where $\beta^2$ drops through zero.

The β²(r) Kottler Profile

The computed β²(r) for a point mass in a Λ>0 background. The inner cliff is the ISCO (relativistic instability); the outer cliff is the cosmological horizon (Λ-unbinding). Bound orbits exist only on the mesa. Drag the Λ slider to see how the outer cliff moves.

The inner cliff, at the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO), is where the relativistic $r^{-3}$ correction overwhelms the Newtonian potential. Below this radius, $\beta^2 < 0$: the radial frequency becomes imaginary, perturbations grow exponentially, and the particle plunges inward. This is the threshold of black hole physics.

The outer cliff, at $r_\Lambda \sim (GM/\Lambda c^2)^{1/3}$, is where the cosmological repulsion overwhelms gravitational binding. Beyond this radius, no stable circular orbits exist as the universe is expanding too fast for gravity to hold on.

Gravitationally bound orbits exist only on the mesa. The inner cliff is general relativity; the outer cliff is cosmology.

Between these two cliffs which span some twenty orders of magnitude in radius for a typical galaxy, the system is a deformed version of the Kepler fixed point. $\beta$ sits near 1, orbits are nearly closed, and the departure from closure is the precession that carries physical information.

Part IV

The Galactic Ridge

The Kottler mesa describes a point mass in vacuum. Real galaxies have extended mass distributions, and this modifies the mesa's shape. As one moves outward through a galaxy, the enclosed mass grows (rather than being constant), and the effective force law shifts from $1/r^2$ toward $1/r$. The corresponding $\beta$ value is:

Logarithmic potential (flat rotation curves)
$$\beta^2 = 2, \qquad \beta = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$$

So the flat-rotation regime does not sit at $\beta = 0$ (a common misconception corrected in this paper) but at $\beta = \sqrt{2}$: between the two Bertrand fixed points. Since $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational, orbits in the outer galaxy are bounded rosettes that never close. The mesa acquires a gentle upward slope, a ridge rising above the Kepler plateau before the $\Lambda$-cliff eventually reasserts itself.

The MOND interpolation, with its characteristic acceleration scale $a_0 \sim 1.2 \times 10^{-10}\;\text{m/s}^2$, governs how steeply $\beta$ climbs from 1 to $\sqrt{2}$. The empirical relation $a_0 \approx \sqrt{\Lambda}\cdot c$ connects this galactic transition to the cosmological constant — the same parameter that controls the outer cliff. Whether this is coincidence or physics remains one of the deepest open questions.

Part V

Below the Mesa

What happens when $\beta^2$ goes negative? The radial perturbation equation becomes $\delta\ddot{r} = +|\Omega_r|^2\,\delta r$: exponential growth rather than oscillation. The effective potential has a maximum rather than a minimum, and any radial perturbation drives the particle away from its circular orbit.

At the ISCO (inner cliff), the particle plunges inward toward the singularity. At the cosmological horizon (outer cliff), it is swept outward by the Hubble flow. Both are regions where Bertrand's classification breaks down entirely — the concept of orbital closure becomes meaningless when orbits are unstable.

Effective Potential & Stability

The Schwarzschild effective potential (blue) with circular orbit marked (dot). Below the ISCO at r = 6rₛ, the effective potential has a maximum, not a minimum: the orbit is unstable. The inset shows the local curvature, which determines the sign of β².

Bertrand's theorem, viewed through the $\beta$ parameter, transforms a classical result about orbit closure into a diagnostic that spans all of gravitational physics. The mesa-plain picture of a Kepler plateau bounded by relativistic and cosmological cliffs, with a galactic ridge in between and so organises everything from Mercury's precession to the expansion of the universe into a single framework. Integrability, and its controlled failure, is the organising principle.
